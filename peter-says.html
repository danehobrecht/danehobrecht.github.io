BACKGROUND AND SIGNIFICANCE

A low-stimulus environment is characterized by the minimal sensory stimuli and the lack of interactions due to social isolations. These factors can cause the development of delirium, the state of confusion and reduced consciousness and cognitive abilities, often leading to other serious complications. Studies actually show that one and every three older adults who experience delirium in the hospital will have another episode after discharge which will lead to hospital readmission. In an enviroment with low sensory stimuli such as visual, auditory, and isolation will cause disorientation and confusion as the brain relies on a steady flow of sensory information to maintain cognitive function and awareness. The lack of social interaction reduces the essential cognitive stimulation resulting in individuals experiencing anxiety and depression which can contribute to delerium. Cognitive tests are an essential tool to help assess the mental status and congition of patients. They can help with the diagnosis and identification of any cognitivie or neurological imparements, delirium, or even dementia. There are many tests that are used such as the mini- mental state examination (MMSE) for orientation and memory, Montreal cognitive assessment (MoCA) for memory and language, and Confusion Assessment Method (CAM) for delirium and confusion. These tests allow physicians and healthcare professionals to monitor any mental or physical cognitive changes that can be caused by factors like infections, side effects of medications or anesthesia, or age. Identifying these underlying conditions in an efficient manner can help improve the overall healthcare quality and patient outcomes. The problem of delirium is intellectually interesting as it shows significant clinical relevance. Up to 62% of patients discharged from the ICU have significant cognitive impairment that is “not associated with severity of illness, number of comorbidities, or length of hospital stay1.” Researchers advocated that “assessing patients’ orientation to person, place, time, and situation and their ability to follow simple single-step commands like squeezing a hand is a great start to identifying obvious cognitive deficits” but subtle changes to cognitive function require different assessment tools that are not implemented regularly in hospitals1. It’s especially important to monitor delirium in dementia patients. It’s found that delirium development in dementia patients may accelerate the trajectory of their cognitive decline and result in long lengths of stay, readmissions, premature nursing home placement or death2. Delirium also proves to affect the care and performance hospitals are evaluated on. It’s associated with many adverse outcomes like increased mortality rates, falls, functional decline, cognitive impairment and decline, and significant costs3. It also puts a major financial burden as medical services and costs range from $38 to $152 billion per year nationwide4. Addressing delirium in the clinical setting is of interest to many hospitals in order to prevent adverse health outcomes and unnecessary medical and administrative costs during a patient’s stay.
